Divergent Plates

Divergent boundaries happen when two plate boundries move away from each other, allowing magma to rise and create new crust These boundaries are often found along mid-ocean ridges, where they produce younger oceanic crust, about 0–4 million years old. Volcanic activity at these boundaries is scattered and diffuse and earthquakes at these boundaries are typically shallow (0–30 km depth). Additionally the depth of the sea floor at these boundaries range from -50 to -3,500 meters.

Although it is not solely divergent, one of the most famous examples of a divergent boundary is the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. This is where the Eurasian and North American plates are slowly moving apart.

Transform Plates

Transform boundaries when two plates slide past each other horizontally. These boundries can cause frequent shallow earthquakes around 0–50 km in depth due to the friction between the plates. While transform boundaries may have some more concentrated volcanic activity, it isn't a lot. The age of the oceanic crust near the boundaries can range from 0 to 40 million years, with the depth of the sea floor generally between -500 and -350 meters.

A well-known example of a transform boundary is the San Andreas Fault in California, where the Pacific Plate and North American Plate slide past each other, causing frequent seismic activity.

Convergent Plates

Convergent boundaries occur where two plates move toward each other. If one plate is denser (usually oceanic plates), it subducts beneath the other, creating trenches. On land, these boundaries result in mountain ranges. The oceanic crust at these boundaries is typically older, around 130–180 million years. Seismic and volcanic activity is intense, with volcanic arcs and earthquakes around 300–700 km in depth

Places like the Andes Mountains in South America and the Cascade Range in the Pacific Northwest of the United States are great example of convergent boundries where both the plates are land. In cases where there is one plate subducting under the other, The Mariana Trench is an example. These trenches can be extremely deep, ranging from 8,000 to 11,000 meters.
